Self-Balancing Autonomous Robot

The goal of this project is to make a platform that I can use to implement what I am learning in a computer vision course that I am taking online. As well as, use the concepts of the control theory and autonomous robotics that I learned at school, and hopefully expand upon what I have previously learned.

I will want it to perform some tasks using computer vision, which is why I selected the Raspberry Pi to act as the brains of the robot. The other microcontroller I have is an arduino, but it wouldn’t be practical to attempt any image processing on this platform. It is also favourable to use the Pi, since most navigating and path planning algorithms would most likely be more than the Arduino could handle.

The mechanical design is complete, the robot will use 2 stepper motors as its means of locomotion. Typically small robots like will use simple DC motors as they are far more efficient, don’t require any power when sitting idle, and have a wider range of rpm. However this robot will be used as an autonomous platform, thus the displacement information provided by the “ticks” of the stepper motor will be very useful. DC motors can accomplish this if they have an encoder attached to them, but this increases the price significantly. You could approximate the distance travelled and use other sensors to correct for the error, however I will be doing this even with the stepper since there will be slippage. The only difference will be that the stepper estimates will be more accurate. Another consideration is that stepper motors aren’t nearly as good at acceleration or variable loads, but this robot won’t need either of these since the load will be relatively constant and won’t require large acceleration.

To drive the motors I will be using TI’s DRV8823. It’s a simple driver that can operate 2 stepper motors (has 4 H-bridges). To communicate with it the Raspberry Pi will need to use its SPI bus. An advantage of the SPI bus is the number of wires are greatly reduced as you can see below when comparing the SPI driver to a DIR/STEP driver.

The trade-off is that a 16bit SPI transaction takes much longer that toggling a couple GPIOs, but the timescale of this is negligible compared to the response time of a stepper motor. I opted to make a custom board for the driver, since I enjoy designing PCBs.

The robot’s sensory information will be provided via 3 different sensors. An ultrasonic range finder will be used for collision avoidance, and can be used in conjunction with a camera or on its own for crude environment mapping. A simple IMU, comprised of 3 axis gyroscope, 3 axis accelerometer, and 3 axis magnetometer, will be used in the control loop to provide the robot’s balancing capability. It will also be used in conjunction with the stepper motor’s displacement data to maintain accurate positioning. The system will most likely use a kalman filter to achieve its dead reckoning navigation. Finally, the robot will have a camera to perform various computer vision tasks using openCV.

Lego Man Retrieval Robot

The objective was to create a device that could retrieve a skier (played by a lego man) stranded on the opposite side of a mountain. The device needed to autonomously navigate the course, find and return the lego to the starting point.

Our solution was to make a vehicle with tracks that could climb the “mountain” which was in reality a wooden ramp with a 45 deg incline; this posed a problem since with normal means of traction this is too steep to climb. Another problem was following the ramp to avoid falling off. We devised an ingenious solution that would solve both problems.

This is a frontal view of the wheel design. It has sewing needles protruding from the inner wheel (smaller diameter) to give maximum grip into the wooden ramp, and has a ridge that allows a belt to transfer power from the rear wheels to the ones in the front. The outer wheel (larger diameter) is used to protect the spikes from being dulled when on the concrete floor, and serves as a guide when going up the ramp. The outer wheel also has a ridge to add a rubber tire to increase traction against smooth concrete. As you can see in the following video the wheel design works well, as we were the only group to successfully climb the ramp.

The second part of the challenge was to devise a method of capturing the lego and holding it. The lego was also placed on a wooden platform (1” plywood), thus we also needed a way of climbing onto it. We once again decided to have a single design to acomplish several tasks. Our design was inspired by bobcat bulldozer; these small machines often use their shovel not only for it’s obvious function, but also to lift itself above obstacles. To ensure that the lego would fall into the shovel we added a sweeping arm to knock the lego into place.

The final challenge was to add a sensor that would enable this robot to operate autonomously. First, we needed a way to have the robot find its location relative to the course. The simplest way would be to use the largest obstacle in the course, the large wall separating both sides of the course. By using a simple distance measurement to this wall you can establish your orientation and distance relative to its plane. Scanning till the edge of the wall will finalize your position. To improve accuracy with cheap and noisy sensors we used linear regression to identify the orientation of the wall. To help reduce the the error in our sensors the laser range sensors were mounted to a panning servo, as moving the entire robot would introduce more error into the measurements. This would identify the robot’s positioning at the time of the scan, but between scans we implemented dead reckoning with the use of a gyroscope, accelerometer, and empirical characterisation of the drive servos to update the robot’s location tracking. The panning sensor was equipped with a long range sensor used for positioning relative to the course and a short range sensor used to determine the position of the lego as it is approaching it.

Six Degrees of Freedom Controller

In 3D space, there are 6 degrees of freedom for any object, 3 translational degrees and 3 rotational. To control such a device the typical way of doing this is to use 2 joysticks, each offering control over 3 degrees. However, this isn’t as intuitive as manipulating the object directly.

To simplify the problem, we chose to have different mechanisms to read the two separate types of motion. The first was the translational movements, which we used a gantry system to track the input. In order for the system to be operated smoothly we used linear rails used for CNC machines. The thing that set this design apart from other 6 degrees of freedom controller was the ability to choose between, continuous motion like you have with a mouse, or return to centre motion like you have with a joystick. The former is useful when controlling a robotic arm that has limited range, the latter can be used when controlling something like a submarine, which can go forward nearly indefinitely. To switch between these two option you only needed to flip the black tabs one each axis as seen in the second image below. The tab has springs on either side which push the axis back to its centre once the user releases the tension, like a joystick.

The rotational motions were much more difficult to track, though it is simple to implement a device with 3 degrees of rotational movement, but we wanted the device to feels as though you were simply manipulating an object in your hand. To do this your hand would have to be at the centre of all three axis of rotation. We settle on a ball and socket type mechanism, but the ball would be hollowed out in order for your hand to be at the centre of rotation. The exterior of the socket having a triangular appearance because each of the points of the triangle has a spring connected to the moving part. This is to give it the same return to center behaviour that the linear axis can can achieve.

The mechanical system is complete, however in order for this to be a controller the system needs sensors to convert the users motion to something meaningful to the device being operated. Most controllers use magnets as a transducer, but thumbsticks have a very small range of motion that can be captured by a single pair of hall effect sensors and a small magnet. This would be feasible for the axis of rotation, but the translational axis would require a different solution. In order to cover the entire range of motion we used a series of hall effect sensors that would stay stationary and the magnet would move relative to its axis. As you can see in the image below the green boards each have a hall effect and are spaced such that as one sensor is about to lose the magnet, another begins detecting it.

The device needs a controller to read the sensors and transmit the data. We opted to make a custom circuit board since it would suit our needs better, and would be a great experience. Being that I was the only one with hardware design experience I was the sole designer of the controller board. Since there were too many sensors to have an ADC for each, and cable management would be difficult on a device with so many moving parts, we used a MUX in each translational axis and one for the rotational axis. The TI MSP430 was selected as the mircocontroller, as it had good documentation, but mostly because TI give free samples! You can see the board schematic below.

Designing the board layout was far more complicated as the board needed to be fairly small and discrete. To make matters worse, at the time of designing the board we couldn’t decide whether we would use hall effect sensors which required analog signals or optical sensors which used SPI, so the board needed to support both in case we changed our minds. The board had connectors dedicated to each of the two options. In the end the hall effect sensor was selected as the method of choice, and the SPI connectors weren’t populated.The image below is an image of the board layout, as you can see this is a dense layout, and required 4 layers.

The device is much smaller than you would think based on the image; the entire mechanism could fit within 10x10 inches. This was done because we wanted this device to take similar real estate on a desk as a mouse pad would.

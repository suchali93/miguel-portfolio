Self-Balancing Autonomous Robot

The goal of this project is to make a platform that I can use to implement what I am learning in an online course on computer vision, as well as expand upon the concepts of control theory and autonomous robotics that I previously learned at school.

Since I want the robot to perform tasks using computer vision, I selected the Raspberry Pi to act as the brain. The other microcontroller I have is an arduino, but it wouldn’t be practical to attempt any image processing on this platform. It is also favourable to use the Pi, since most navigating and path planning algorithms would most likely be more than the Arduino could handle.

So far, I have worked on the mechanical design which is now complete. The robot uses 2 stepper motors as its means of locomotion. Typically, small robots like this use simple DC motors as they are far more efficient, don’t require any power when sitting idle, and have a wider range of rpm. However, this robot will be used as an autonomous platform; thus, the displacement information provided by the “ticks” of the stepper motor will be very useful. DC motors can accomplish this if they have an encoder attached to them, but this increases the price significantly. You could approximate the distance travelled and use other sensors to correct for the error, however, since there will be slippage, I am going to be doing this even with the stepper. The only difference is that the stepper estimates will be more accurate. Another consideration is that stepper motors aren’t nearly as good at acceleration or variable loads, but this robot doesn't need either of these since the load will be relatively constant and won’t require large acceleration.

To drive the motors I am using TI’s DRV8823. It’s a simple driver that can operate 2 stepper motors (has 4 H-bridges). In order to communicate with it, the Raspberry Pi uses its SPI bus. An advantage of the SPI bus is that the number of wires is greatly reduced, as you can see below, when comparing the SPI driver to a DIR/STEP driver.

The trade-off is that a 16bit SPI transaction takes much longer than toggling a couple GPIOs, but the timescale of this is negligible as compared to the response time of a stepper motor. I opted to make a custom board for the driver, since I enjoy designing PCBs.

The robot’s sensory information is provided via 3 different sensors. An ultrasonic range finder is used for collision avoidance, and can be used in conjunction with a camera or on its own for crude environment mapping. A simple IMU, comprised of 3 axis gyroscope, 3 axis accelerometer, and 3 axis magnetometer, is used in the control loop to provide the robot’s balancing capability. It will also be used in conjunction with the stepper motor’s displacement data to maintain accurate positioning. The system will most likely use a kalman filter to achieve its dead reckoning navigation. Finally, the robot will have a camera to perform various computer vision tasks using openCV.

Lego Man Retrieval Robot

The objective of this project was to create a device that could retrieve a skier (lego man) stranded on the opposite side of a mountain (a wooden ramp with a 45° incline). The device needed to autonomously navigate the course, find and retrieve the lego, and then return to the starting point.

Our solution was to make a robot with tracks that could climb the ramp. However, we were faced with a couple of problems. First, the 45° angle was too steep to climb with normal means of traction. Second, without proper guides, the robot would fall off the ramp. So, we devised an ingenious solution that would solve both problems.

This is a frontal view of the wheel design. It has sewing needles protruding from the inner wheel (smaller diameter) to give maximum grip into the wooden ramp, and has a ridge that allows a belt to transfer power from the rear wheels to the ones in the front. The outer wheel (larger diameter) is used to protect the spikes from being dulled when on the concrete floor, and serves as a guide when going up the ramp. The outer wheel also has a ridge to add a rubber tire to increase traction against smooth concrete. The following video shows that the wheel design worked well, and the robot successfully climbed the ramp.

The second part of the challenge was to devise a method of capturing the lego and holding it. The lego was placed on a 1 inch tall wooden platform, so we needed a way of climbing onto that as well. We, once again, decided to have a single design to acomplish several tasks. Our design was inspired by bobcat bulldozer; these small machines often use their shovel not only for it’s obvious function, but also to lift itself above obstacles. To ensure that the lego would fall into the shovel we added a sweeping arm to knock the lego into place.

The final challenge was to add a sensor that would enable this robot to operate autonomously. We needed a way to have the robot find its location relative to the course. The simplest way was to use the largest obstacle in the course — the large wall separating both sides of the course. By using a simple distance measurement to this wall we established the robot's orientation and distance relative to its plane. Scanning till the edge of the wall finalized its position. To improve accuracy with cheap and noisy sensors, we used linear regression to identify the orientation of the wall. To help reduce the the error in our sensors, the laser range sensors were mounted to a panning servo, as moving the entire robot would introduce more error into the measurements. This identified the robot’s positioning at the time of the scan, but between scans, we implemented dead reckoning with the use of a gyroscope, accelerometer, and empirical characterisation of the drive servos to update the robot’s location tracking. The panning sensor was equipped with a long range sensor used for positioning relative to the course and a short range sensor used to determine the position of the lego as the robot approached it.

Six Degrees of Freedom Controller

In 3D space, there are 6 degrees of freedom for any object — 3 translational degrees and 3 rotational. Typically, to control these 6 degrees, two joysticks are used, each offering control over 3 degrees. However, this isn’t as intuitive as manipulating the object directly.

To simplify the problem, we chose to have different mechanisms to read the two separate types of motion. The first was the translational movements; we used a gantry system to track the input. In order for the system to be operated smoothly, we used linear rails which are used for CNC machines.

What set this design apart from other 6 degrees of freedom controllers, was the ability to choose between continuous motion like with a mouse, or return to centre motion like with a joystick. The former is useful when controlling a robotic arm that has limited range, the latter can be used when controlling something like a submarine which can go forward indefinitely. To switch between these two options, you only needed to flip the black tabs on each axis, as seen in the image below. The tab has springs on either side which push the axis back to its centre once the user releases the tension, like a joystick.

The rotational motions were much more difficult to track. Although, it is simple to implement a device with 3 degrees of rotational movement, we wanted the device to feel as though you were simply manipulating an object in your hand. To do this, your hand would have to be at the centre of all three axes of rotation. So, we settled on a ball and socket type mechanism. The ball is hollowed out in order for the hand to be at the centre of rotation. The exterior of the socket is triangular, and each edge of the triangle has a spring connected to the moving part. This gives it the same return-to-center behaviour that the linear axis can achieve.

That completed the mechanical system. However, in order for this to be a controller the system needed sensors to convert the user's motion to something meaningful for the device. Most controllers use magnets as a transducer, but thumbsticks have a very small range of motion that can be captured by a single pair of hall effect sensors and a small magnet. This was feasible for the axis of rotation, but the translational axis required a different solution. In order to cover the entire range of motion, we used a series of hall effect sensors that would stay stationary and the magnet would move relative to its axis. As you can see in the image below, each of the green boards has a hall effect and they are all spaced such that as one sensor is about to lose the magnet, another begins detecting it.

The device needs a controller to read the sensors and transmit the data. We opted to make a custom circuit board since it would better suit our needs, and would be a great experience. Being the only one with hardware design experience, I was the sole designer of the controller board. Since there were too many sensors to have an ADC for each, and cable management would be difficult on a device with so many moving parts, we used a MUX in each translational axis and one for the rotational axis. The TI MSP430 was selected as the mircocontroller, partly because it had good documentation, but mostly because TI gave free samples! You can see the board schematic below.

Designing the board layout was far more complicated than we amticipated, as the board needed to be fairly small and discrete. To make matters worse, at the time of designing the board, we couldn’t decide whether we would use hall effect sensors, which required analog signals, or optical sensors, which used SPI. So, the board needed to support both in case we changed our minds. The board had connectors dedicated to each of the two options. In the end, we selected the hall effect sensors. As you can see below in the image of the board, this is a dense layout and required 4 layers.

The device is much smaller than you would think based on the image; the entire mechanism fits within 10x10 inches. This was done because the controller is meant to take similar real estate on a desk as a mouse pad would.
